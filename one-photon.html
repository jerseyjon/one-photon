Birth · the core

Forged in a pressure of 250 billion atmospheres

At the centre of the Sun, gravity squeezes hydrogen so hard that protons overcome their mutual repulsion and fuse. Each completed proton–proton chain turns four hydrogen nuclei into one helium nucleus — and the tiny mass left over becomes pure energy.

Our photon is born here as a gamma ray, one flash among an unimaginable torrent: the Sun converts about four million tonnes of matter into light every second.

The long walk · radiative zone

One centimetre at a time, for a hundred thousand years

The core's plasma is so dense that our photon travels roughly a centimetre before being absorbed and re-emitted in a random direction. Then another centimetre. Then another.

This drunkard's walk across 500,000 km of star takes somewhere between 10,000 and 170,000 years. Each collision bleeds away a little energy — the violent gamma ray slowly mellows toward visible light. When our photon finally nears the surface, mammoths may have walked the Earth at its birth.

Escape · photosphere

The last scattering

At the photosphere — the boiling visible surface, tiled with convection granules the size of France — the plasma finally thins enough that light can fly free. One last re-emission, and our photon leaves the Sun forever.

Cooled from gamma ray to a warm yellow-green visible photon, it departs at exactly the speed it was always capable of: 299,792,458 metres per second. The hard part is over. The fast part begins.

Transit · the void

149,600,000 km in 8 minutes 20 seconds

A hundred millennia to cross one star. Eight minutes to cross the gulf to Earth. In vacuum, nothing absorbs, nothing scatters — the photon simply is motion, experiencing (in its own reference frame) no time at all.

It travels in formation with a broader stream delivering 1,361 watts to every square metre that faces the Sun at Earth's distance — the solar constant, the raw energy budget of our entire world.

Arrival · atmosphere

Why the sky turns blue around it

Plunging into air, the photon runs a final gauntlet. Nitrogen and oxygen molecules scatter light in proportion to 1/λ⁴ — Rayleigh scattering — so blue photons ricochet across the whole dome of the sky while our longer-wavelength traveller barrels through nearly straight.

Clouds, dust and air absorb roughly a quarter of the incoming beam. On a clear day, about 1,000 W/m² survives to ground level. Our photon is among the survivors, and below it, tilted to meet it, is a rectangle of dark glass.

Capture · the cell

A 100,000-year journey ends in a nanosecond

The photon strikes a crystalline silicon wafer and dies doing something useful. Its energy — comfortably above silicon's 1.12 eV band gap — knocks an electron out of the crystal lattice, leaving a positively-charged hole behind.

The cell's p–n junction is a one-way electric field: it sweeps the electron toward the front contacts before it can recombine. Pushed through busbars, an inverter, and your consumer unit, that electron joins a current. A modern panel converts 22–24% of incoming light this way — and the kettle switches on.
